# Final Recommendation

## Executive Summary

A regression analysis was conducted to identify the business factors most strongly associated with monthly sales across retail stores. Three regression models were developed and compared, including two simple linear regression models and one multiple linear regression model.

The analysis found that **footfall, inventory availability, and marketing spend** have positive and statistically significant relationships with monthly sales. The final multiple regression model achieved an **R² value of 0.811**, indicating that it explains approximately **81.1%** of the variation in monthly sales. This makes it the most reliable model for understanding store performance and supporting business decisions.

Based on these findings, leadership should focus on increasing customer footfall, maintaining high inventory availability, and investing marketing resources efficiently while using the regression model as a decision-support tool rather than relying on it as the sole basis for decision-making.

---

# Key Factors Associated with Monthly Sales

The analysis identified several variables that are strongly associated with monthly sales.

### 1. Customer Footfall

Customer footfall is the strongest predictor of monthly sales. The regression results show that stores with higher customer traffic consistently achieve higher sales. This suggests that initiatives aimed at attracting more customers into stores are likely to have the greatest impact on sales performance.

Examples of actions include:

* Improving local marketing campaigns.
* Running targeted promotional events.
* Enhancing customer experience to encourage repeat visits.
* Increasing visibility through local advertising and partnerships.

---

### 2. Inventory Availability

Inventory availability also has a positive and statistically significant relationship with monthly sales.

Stores with higher inventory availability are less likely to experience stock-outs, allowing customers to purchase the products they need without interruption.

Leadership should therefore continue investing in effective inventory planning, demand forecasting, and supply chain management to minimise lost sales caused by unavailable products.

---

### 3. Marketing Spend

Marketing spend has a positive relationship with monthly sales, indicating that marketing investments contribute to revenue growth.

However, marketing spend alone explains only a small portion of sales variation compared with footfall. This suggests that marketing is most effective when it successfully drives customer visits rather than simply increasing advertising expenditure.

Marketing budgets should therefore be evaluated based on their ability to generate customer traffic and improve store performance.

---

# Regional Insights

The multiple regression model also examined regional differences.

The analysis found that stores located in the **East region** perform significantly differently from the **West region**, which was used as the reference category in the regression model.

The **North** and **South** regions did not show statistically significant differences after accounting for the other business variables.

This suggests that leadership should investigate the East region further to understand the reasons behind its lower performance. Possible factors include local competition, customer demographics, operational challenges, or regional market conditions that were not captured in the available dataset.

---

# Variables That Should Not Be Over-Interpreted

Not every variable included in the regression model showed a meaningful relationship with monthly sales.

The coefficients for **Region_North** and **Region_South** were not statistically significant, meaning there is insufficient evidence to conclude that these regions perform differently from the West region after considering the other predictors.

For this reason, business decisions should not be based solely on these regional differences.

---

# Recommended Business Actions

Based on the regression analysis, the following actions are recommended:

* Prioritise initiatives that increase customer footfall, as customer traffic has the strongest association with monthly sales.
* Maintain high inventory availability to reduce stock-outs and improve product accessibility.
* Continue investing in marketing campaigns, while measuring their effectiveness through customer visits and sales outcomes rather than advertising spend alone.
* Conduct a detailed operational review of stores in the East region to identify improvement opportunities.
* Continue monitoring store performance using analytical models to support future business decisions.

---

# Risks and Limitations

Although the regression model explains a large proportion of the variation in monthly sales, several limitations should be considered.

The analysis is based only on the variables included in the dataset. Other factors such as pricing strategies, local competition, seasonal demand, weather conditions, economic changes, customer demographics, and store management practices were not available for analysis.

In addition, regression analysis identifies statistical relationships between variables but does not prove that one variable directly causes another. For example, while higher footfall is associated with higher sales, the analysis alone cannot confirm that increasing footfall will always cause sales to increase, since other business factors may also influence both variables.

Therefore, the regression model should be used alongside business knowledge, operational experience, and market research when making strategic decisions.

---

# Final Recommendation

The **multiple regression model** should be adopted as the preferred analytical model for evaluating monthly sales performance because it provides the highest explanatory power (**R² = 0.811**) and considers multiple operational and regional factors simultaneously.

The analysis indicates that leadership should focus primarily on increasing customer footfall, ensuring strong inventory availability, and allocating marketing resources effectively, as these variables demonstrate the strongest positive associations with monthly sales.

At the same time, the findings should be interpreted responsibly. Regression provides valuable evidence about relationships between business variables, but it should complement—not replace—managerial judgement and additional business analysis.

Overall, the results provide strong support for using data-driven decision-making to improve store performance while recognising that continuous monitoring and additional business insights are essential for long-term success.
